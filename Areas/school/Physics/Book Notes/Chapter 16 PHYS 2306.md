- A **wave** is a disturbance traveling through a medium.
- A **transverse wave** is when the displacement is perpendicular to the direction of motion 
- A **longitudinal wave** is a wave in which the displacement is parallel to the direction of travel. 


### Wave properties 
- **Wave** **speed**: How fast it travels through the medium
- **Wavelength:** The distance between two neighboring crests
- **Frequency:** The number of oscillations per second 
- **Amplitude:** The maximum displacement 


- Sound waves are longitudinal waves
- Light waves are transverse waves 


### Wave Energy
- The rate at which a wave delivers energy to a surface is the intensity of the wave. For sound this is the decibel scale. 


### Doppler Effect
- The frequency and wavelength are shifted if there is relative motion beyween the source and the observer this is called the doppler effect


## 16.1 Introduction 
-

- A **transverse wave** is a wave in which the displacement is _perpendicular_ to the direction in which the wave travels. For example, a wave travels along a string in a horizontal direction while the particles that make up the string oscillate vertically. Electromagnetic waves are also transverse waves because the electromagnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction in which the wave travels.
- In a **longitudinal wave**, the particles in the medium are displaced _parallel_ to the direction in which the wave travels. Here we see a chain of masses connected by springs. If you give the first mass in the chain a sharp push, a disturbance travels down the chain by compressing and expanding the springs. Sound waves in gases and liquids are the most well known examples of longitudinal waves.

-  **Mechanical waves** travel within a medium such as air or water
- **Electromagnetic waves**, form radio waves to visible light to x rays are self sustaining oscillation of the electromagnetic field. 


- Medium is the substance which the waves travels through eg water or air 
	- It must be elastic meaning it must return the wave to equilibrium



### 16.1.1 Wave speed

- Once created, the disturbance travels outward through the medium at the wave speed $v$. 
	- This doesnt mean that the wave transfers any material or substance outward from the source

$$
v_{string}=\sqrt{ \frac{T_{s}}{\mu} }
$$
> where $\mu$ is the strings linear density $\mu =m/L$


- Unlike a string in general the wave speed is a property of the medium.

## Doppler Effect
- $v_{s}$ is the speed of the source 
- $f_{+}$ frequency emitted by the approaching source
- $f_{-}$ frequency of the receding source




![[Pasted image 20260219201044.png#invert]]

- because $f=v/\lambda$ as the source moves in one direction the waves are bunched up in the direction that the source is moving.
- This means that the wavelength is different and therefore the frequency changes even if the speed of the wave remains unchanged

$$
\begin{align}
D(x,t)  &  = A\sin\left[ 2\pi\left( \frac{x}{\lambda}-\frac{t}{T} \right) +\phi_{0} \right] \\
 & =A\sin(k-\omega t+\phi_{0})
\end{align}
$$
> Where $A$ is the amplitude, $k=\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number $\omega = 2\pi f=2\pi/T$ is the angular frequency, and, $\phi_{0}$ is the phase constant. 

The fundamental relationship for any sinusoidal wave is $v= \lambda f$
