### 22.1.1 Charge model 

- Frictional forces, such as rubbing, add something called charge to an object or remove it from the object. The process itself is called _charging_. More vigorous rubbing produces a larger quantity of charge.
- There are two and only two kinds of charge. For now we will call these “plastic charge” and “glass charge.” Other objects can sometimes be charged by rubbing, but the charge they receive is either “plastic charge” or “glass charge.”
- Two like charges (plastic/plastic or glass/glass) exert repulsive forces on each other. Two opposite charges (plastic/glass) attract each other.
- The force between two charges is a long-range force. The size of the force increases as the quantity of charge increases and decreases as the distance between the charges increases.
- _Neutral_ objects have an _equal mixture_ of both “plastic charge” and “glass charge.” The rubbing process somehow manages to separate the two.

### 22.4 Coulomb's Law

$$
\mathrm{F_{1 \space on \space2}} = \mathrm{F_{2 \space on \space 1}}
= \frac{K |q_{1}| | q_{2} |}{r^2}
$$
- describes the force between point charges
-

#### 22.4.1 Units of charge 

- the Coulomb is based on $e$ 
$$e =1.6\times10^{-19}C$$
- Given this we define $k$ as
$$
K = 8.99 \times 10^9  \mathrm{\,N\,m^2/C^2}
$$
	- 8.99 is usually rounded to 9

**Permittivity constant $(\epsilon_{0})$** 
$$
\epsilon_{0} = \frac{1}{4\pi K} =8.85 \times 10^{-12} \mathrm{C^2/N \,m^2}
$$

#### 22.42 Using Coulomb's Law

- Coulombs law applies only to point charges.
- Electric forces can be superimposed. Multiple charges give multiple forces. 


### The Electric Field 


- Source charges alter the space around them by creating an electric field $\vec{E}$ at all points in space. 
- A separate $q$ in the electric field experiences force 
$$
\vec{F}_{\text{on $q$}} = q \vec{E}
$$
- in the direction of $\vec{E}$, the force on a negative charge is directed opposite to $\vec{E}$.

- lets define vector $\hat{r}$ to be a vector of length 1 (unit vector) that points in the direction of the electric field 

**Electric field of a point charge**
$$
\vec{E} = \frac{1}{\pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$
> where $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector from the charge toward the point at which we want to know the field
