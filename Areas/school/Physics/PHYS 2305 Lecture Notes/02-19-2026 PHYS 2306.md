## Models for E fields based on source 
&emsp;


### (1) small charge 
$$
 E= \frac{kq}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$
> $r$ is the difference between the charge and the point  
> $\hat{r}$ is a unit vector parallel to $r$


&emsp;

###  (2) Rod Line Wire 

$$
\vec{E} = \frac{kQ}{x\left( x^2+\frac{L^2}{4} \right)^{1/2}}
$$
Where $\lambda = \frac{Q}{L}$

- What is $L?$

#### (2b) Long Rod $(x\ll L)$ or $L\to \infty$
$$
\vec{E} = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi \epsilon_{0}x} \hat{i}
$$

&emsp;
###  (3) Ring of charge 
$$
\lambda = \frac{Q}{2\pi R}
$$
$$
\vec{E} \frac{kQx}{(x^2+R^2)^{3/2}}
$$
> R is the radius of the ring 
> $x$ is the distance from the ring to the field 

&emsp;


### (4) Disk (sheet, plate)

$$
\zeta = \frac{Q}{\pi R^2}
$$
$$
\vec{E} =\frac{\zeta}{2 \epsilon_{0}} \left[ 1-\frac{x}{\sqrt{ R^2+x^2 }} \right] \hat{i}
$$
> With radius $R$

- Note that if you are very close to a plate it may be considered a large plate 


#### (4B) large disk  $(x \ll R)$ or $(R \to \infty)$

$$
| \vec{E} | = \frac{\zeta}{2\epsilon_{0}}
$$
> the $\vec{E}$ field is perpendicular to the disk 



&emsp;



#### (5) Spherical Charge

#### On the surface
$$
\zeta = \frac{Q}{4\pi R^2}
$$
- Like with single point charge 
$$
\vec{E} = \frac{kQ}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$
> where $r$ is the distance from the origin 


#### In the volume 

$$
 \vec{E}_{\mathrm{out}} =\frac{kQ}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$
$$\vec{E}_{\mathrm{in}}=\frac{kQr}{R^3}=\frac{pr}{3\epsilon_{0}}$$





&emsp;



### Constant $k$ vs $\epsilon_{0}$


$$
K=\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}} = 9 \cdot 10^{9}
$$
- We can use either $k$ or $\epsilon_{0}$ but we must convert from one to the other 


&emsp;



## Examples

> [!Example]- **Example 1:** Find the electrical field along a disk charge.
> 
> A $35 \mu C$ charge is spread uniformly across the surface of a circular disk whose center is perpendicular to the $x$ axis. the disk has a radius of $R=9.4 \mathrm{ cm}$ find the electrical field at a point along the axis of the disk at a distance $x = R/\sqrt{ 3 }$ from its center.
> 
> 
> $$
> \begin{align}
> \vec{E}  & = \frac{\zeta}{2\epsilon_{0}} \left[ 1-\frac{1}{\sqrt{ 1+R^2/x^2 }} \right] \\
>  
>  & = \frac{\zeta}{2\epsilon_{0}}\left[ 1- \frac{1}{\sqrt{ 1+R^2/(R^2/3) }} \right] \\
>   
>   & = \frac{\zeta}{2\epsilon_{0}}\left[ 1-\frac{1}{2} \right]  \\
 & =\frac{\zeta}{4\epsilon_{0}}
> \end{align}
> $$
> 
> $$
> \boxed{\vec{E}     =   {\frac{\zeta}{4\epsilon_{0}}}} 
> 
> $$
> 


> [!EXAMPLE]- **Example 2:** Given a semicircle of charge find the electric field generated at the origin
> 
> Consider a uniformly charged thin rod bent into a semicircle of radius $R$. Find the electric field generated at the origin of the coordinate system. 
> 
> 
> 
> - Charge per unit length: $\lambda = Q/\pi R$
> 
> 
> $$
> \begin{array}{ll}
> \lambda = \frac{Q}{\pi r},  & R=r
> \end{array}
> $$
> 
> $$
> \begin{align}
> \vec{E}   & = \int_{0}^{\pi} |\phi   \vec{E} | \sin(\phi)   \\
>  & =\int_{0}^\pi \frac{k \lambda \cancel R}{R^{\cancel 2}} \, d\phi \\
>  & =\frac{k\lambda}{R}[-\cos(\phi)]_{0}^\pi \\
>  & =\frac{2k\lambda}{R}
> \end{align}
> $$
> $$
> \boxed{\vec{E} = \frac{2k\lambda}{R}}
> $$
>
