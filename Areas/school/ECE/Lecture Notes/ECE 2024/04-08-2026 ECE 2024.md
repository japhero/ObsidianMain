## Caps with diodes
- We have a paralell cap with the load and a diode from the source this causes the cap to charge when the diode is in the positive cycle and then while the diode is off it discharges
![[Pasted image 20260408113122.png#invert|400]]
- Note this is a balance in between a couple factors 
	- We want the charge time to be less than the diode on time because then the cap wont be charged when the diode turns off
	- 

![[Pasted image 20260408113221.png#invert|400]]
- For a large cap the diode will be off for most of the time the charge loss by the cap when the diode is not conducting can be approximated with
$$
Q \cong I_{L}T
$$


### Ripple current/voltage
- The ripple voltage/current is seen by the load is a function of the cap and the amount of charge q removed by the load 

$$
\begin{align}
V_{r}=\frac{Q}{C},  &  &  C = \frac{Q}{V_{r}},  &  & C = \frac{I_{L}T}{V_{r}}
\end{align}
$$

- The average DC voltage delivered to the load is equal to the peak AC voltage minus half the ripple
$$
V_{L} = V_{m} - \frac{V_{r}}{2}
$$

**Aditional Considerations**
- Putting the cap in parallel on the other side of the diode stresses the cap because when the diode turns off we get a negative voltage on the anode. This voltage is called the peak inverse voltage

### Full Bridge Rectifier 

- We use 4 diodes instead of 2 or 1 
![[Pasted image 20260408115018.png#invert|400]]

- D1 and D2 are on when we have a positive cycle 
- D4 and D3 are on when we have a negative cycle

- another advantage of full bridge rectifiers are that the cap doesn't have to be as big because the cap only maintains the load voltage for 1/4 the wave period. 

- This means we can write the smoothing capacitor equation as 
$$
C = \frac{I_{L}T}{2V_{r}}
$$
