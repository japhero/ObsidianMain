 - The study of the changes for instance the changes defined by derivatives in the current and voltage equations for inductors and capacitors.

**Transient Terminology**
- $0+$ means "an instant **after** the switch opens"
- $0-$ means "an instance **before** the switch opens"

**First order homogenous differential equation for RC circuits**
$$
RC \frac{dv_{c}(t)}{dt} + v_{c}(t)=0
$$

**First order homogenous differential equation for RL circuits**
$$
\frac{L}{R} \frac{di_{L}(t)}{dt}+i_{L}(t)=0
$$


- From diff eq class we know that this type of homogenous differential equation an exponential solution of the form 
$$
f(t)=Ke^{st}
$$
- Exponential functions like the one above are called eigenfunctions.
- If we take the derivative we get 
$$
\frac{df(t)}{dt}= Kse^{St}
$$
Solving specifically for the voltage in RC equations 
$$
v_{c}(t)=Ke^{\frac {-1}{RC}t}
$$
> where $K$ is the voltage at time zero 

Solving specifically for the current in the RL circuit we get 
$$
i_{L}(t)=i_{L}(0)e^{\frac {-R}{L} t}
$$
- The reason all of the discharge equations have negative exponents is because as both the inductor and the capacitor discharges they lose either their voltage or current at an exponential rate. They are discharged through a resistor.

### Time constants $(\tau)$

- The time $\tau =RC$ is known as the time constant of a RC circuit 
	- it takes the voltage magnitude to drop to $36.8 \%$ of its innital value 
- The time $\tau=L/R$ is known as the time constant of an RL circuit
	- it is the time it takes the current magnitude to drop $36.8 \%$ of its initial value

- The time constant only exists during the transient
