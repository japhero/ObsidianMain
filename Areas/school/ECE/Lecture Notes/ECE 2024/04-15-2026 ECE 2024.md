### Small signal model 

- is a linear approximation model that we can apply to non linear components 
	- First we use load line analysis to determine the DC operating quiescent (Q) point
	- Then we use the small signal model to analyze the operation of the small AC signal around that operating Q point. 

- A little change in voltage results in a large change in current 
	- Basically we go up on the slope of the operating curve which is exponental and the larger the slope it means that given a smaller variation in voltage we get a large change in voltage. 

![[Pasted image 20260415113002.png#invert|center|400]]
> $V_{D_{Q}}(t)$ is the DC diode voltage at a specific operating Q point
> $V_{d}(t)$ is the "small-signal" diode voltage: the small variation of the diode voltage around the specific operating current $v_{D_{Q}}$
> $V_{D}(t)$ is the total diode voltage, DC + AC components
> We have the same terminology with $I_{D_{Q}},I_{d},I_{D}$

- For small variations, close to the operating point we assume the characteristic is linear 

$$
\left( \frac{d_{i_{d}}(t)}{dv_{d}(t)} \right)_{Q}= \frac{\Delta i_{d}}{\Delta v_{D}}
$$

![[Pasted image 20260415113718.png#invert|center|300]]
- This assumed linear behavior can be modeled by a small resistance. We call it the Dynamic Resistance: 
$$
r_{d}= \frac{\Delta v_{D}}{\Delta i_{D}}
$$
- To get the dynamic resistance with the Shockley diode equation we take the derivative and solve giving
$$
r_{d}=\frac{i_{D_{Q}}}{nV_{T}}
$$
