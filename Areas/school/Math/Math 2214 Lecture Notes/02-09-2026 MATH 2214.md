## Existence and uniqueness THRM for second order diff eq



> [!Definition] Existence and uniqueness theorem for second order differential Equations
> 
> $$
> y''+p(t)y'+q(t)y=g(t)
> $$
> 
> 
> If $p(t),q(t),g(t)$ are continuous on some interval $a,b$ and $t_{0} \in (a,b)$ then the above initial value problem has a unique solution on $(a,b)$
> 



## Section 3.2 

- The principal of superposition
- This section is about second order linear homogenous equations.

$$
 y''+p(t)y'+q(t)y=0
$$

If $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ are solutions to the above equation then $c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2}$ are solutions too for any real number $C_{1}$ or $C_{2}$.

$$
\begin{align}
 & (c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2})'' +p(c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2})' + q(c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2}) \\
 = & c_{1}y_{1}''+c_{2}y_{2}''+c_{1}py_{1}'+c_{2}py_{2}'+c_{1}qy_{1}+c_{2}qy_{2} \\
 = &  c_{1}(y_{1}''+py_{1}'+qy_{1}) +c_{2}(y_{2}''+py_{1}'+qy_{2})
\end{align}
$$

> [!Definition]
> Assume $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ are the solution of the equation
> $$
>  y''+p(t)y'+q(t)y=0
> $$
> 
> If every solution $y$ of this equation can be written as $y=c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2}$ then we call $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ a fundamental set of solutions.
> 
> > Where a fundamental set of solutions is practically the same thing as a basis 
> > 



### Matrix form of constants 


- Given we take the derivative of $y=c_{1}y_{1}(t_{0}) +c_{2}y_{2}(t_{0})=y_{0}$
	- We get the matrix below 

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
y_{1}(t_{0}) & y_{2}(t_{0}) \\
y_{1}'(t_{0}) & y_{2}'(t_{0})
\end{bmatrix}

\begin{bmatrix}
c_{1} \\
c_{2}
\end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix}
y_{0} \\
y_{0}'
\end{bmatrix}
$$

We want to determine if it has a unique solution so we take the determinant and make sure it is non-zero 


$$
W(y_{1},y_{2}) =
\left |
\begin{array}{ll}
y_{1}(t_{0}) & y_{2}(t_{0}) \\
y_{1}'(t_{0}) & y_{2}'(t_{0})
\end{array}
\right | \not= 0
$$
> This is called the Wronskian determinant

- We use the Wronskian determinant to figure out weather or not we have a unique solution for a given second order differential. 

- When we get a nonconstant result from the Wronskian determinant we test it on the interval of the differential equation 
	- For example if the Wronskian determinant is $-4t$ then we must test $-4t$ on the interval that $p(t)$ and $q(t)$ exist and see if $-4t =0$ on that interval. If it doesn't then we know that its a unique solution. 



> [!Example]- 
> 
> 
> $$
> \begin{array}{ll}
> y_{1} = \cos (2t) \\
> y_{2} = \sin(2t)
> \end{array}
> $$
> 
> 
> take the Wronskian
> 
> $$
> W(y_{1},y_{2})=
> 
> \left |
> \begin{array}{ll}
> \cos(2t) & \sin (2t) \\
> 2\sin(2t) & 2\cos(2t)
> \end{array}
> \right | =
> 
> 2\cos(2t)^2 +2\sin(2t)^2
> 
> $$
> From trig we know that $\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1$ so 
> 
> $$
> \begin{align}
> W(y_{1},y_{2}) & = 2(\cos(2t)^2+\sin(2t)^2) \\
>  & =2(1) \\
>  & =2\not=0
> \end{align}
> $$
> therefore we know it is a unique solution
> 


## Section 3.2 Solving Linear Homogenous Second Order Differential Equations with Constant Coefficients 

Using the form 
$$
ay''+by'+cy=0
$$
$$
\begin{align}
y & = e^{\lambda t}  \\
y' & =\lambda e^{\lambda t} \\
y'' & =\lambda^2e^{\lambda t}

\end{align}
$$
Substitute for the initial equation

$$
\begin{align}
0 & =a\lambda^2e^{\lambda t} +b\lambda e^{\lambda t} + ce^{\lambda t} \\
 & =e^{\lambda t}(\lambda^2a+\lambda b+c)

\end{align}
$$

where $\lambda^2a+\lambda b+c$ is a characteristic polynomial 

Now plug back in 

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
 y_{1} = e^{\lambda_{1}t} \\
y_{2}= e^ {\lambda_{2}t} 
\end{array}
$$




#### Example

Solve
$$
y''+8y'+15y=0
$$

We plug in to solve for the characteristic polynomial 
$$
\begin{align} 0  & =
\lambda^2+8\lambda +15   \\
 & =(\lambda+3)(\lambda+5)
\end{align}

$$
$$
\lambda_{1} = -3,\lambda_{2} = -5
$$
Solve for $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$

$$
y_{1}=e^{-3t},y_{2 }=e^{-5t}
$$
If we wanted we could take the Wronskian to check but lets assume that its a good solution then we get 

$$
y=C_{1}e^{-3t}+C_{2}e^{-5t}
$$
