$$

$$

$$
\begin{array}{ll} 
y_{1}=e^{\lambda_{1} t}, & y_{2} = e^{\lambda_{2}t}

### Reduction of Order

- It is the idea that we can represent multiple solutions by multiplying a solution by some arbitrary function that hopefully gives us the other one. 
	- This idea is then applied to the order where we solve first order to find second
	- 


> [!EXample]- Example of reduction of order
> **Example**
> - Given the equation 
> $$
> ty'' - (2t+1)y'+(t+1)y = 0
> $$
> - $y_{1}=e^t$ is a solution that satisfies the condition given by the equation
> 
> - Reduction of order states that we want to represent more solutions to the equation as a product of two functions 
> $$
> y_{2} = u(t)y_{1}(t) =ue^t
> $$
> If we solve for $y_{2}''$ and $y_{2}''$ we can substitute back into the original second order equation
> $$
> \begin{align} 0 & =
> t(u''+2u'+u)e^t-(2t+1)(u'+u)e^t+(t+1)ue^t\\
>  & =(tu'' +  { \cancel{2tu'+tu;-2tu}-u' \cancel{-u+tu+u}})e^t \\
>  & =(tu''-u')e^t
> \end{align}
> $$
> - Within the above equation we know that $e^t$ will not be zero so therefore the equation in the parentheses must be zero
> $$
> tu''-u'=0
> $$
> write $u'=v$
> $$
> \begin{align}
> t \frac{dv}{dt} =v \\
> \frac{dv}{v}=\frac{dt}{t} \\
> \int v^{-1} \, dv = \int \frac{1}{t}  \, dt \\
> \ln(v)=\ln(t) \\
> v=t  
> \end{align}
> $$
> plug back in the identity giving 
> $$
> \begin{align}
> u' & =t \\
> u & =\frac{1}{2}t^2 \\ \\
> y_{2} & =t^2e^t
> \end{align}
> $$
