## Section 3.11 nth Order Differential Equations
$$
y^{n}+P_{n-1}(t)y^{n-1}+\dots+P_{2}(t)y''+P_{1}(t)y'+P_{0}(t)y=g(t) \tag{*}
$$
> $(*)$ will refer to this equation when it is homogenous 
- We need $n$ number of initial conditions to solve this nth order differential equation
$$
y(t_{0})=y_{0},y'(t_{0})=y_{0}',\dots,y^{(n-1)}(t_{0})=y^{(n-1)}_{0}
$$
**Existence and uniqueness**

- IF $P_{n-1}(t)$ and $P_{n-2}$ and $\dots P_{0}(t) \text{ and } g(t)$ are continuous on the interval $(a,b)$ and $t\in(a,b)$ then the above initial value problem has a unique solution on $(a,b)$.

- If $y_{1},\dots ,y_{r}$ are solutions of * then $c_{1}y_{1}+\dots+c_{r}y$ is also a solution of *.
	- IF $*$ is a homogenous equation so $g(t)=0$



> [!definition] Def for fundamental set of solutions for nth order differential equation.
> ${y_{1},y_{2},\dots,y_{n}}$ forms a fundamental set of solutions for $(*)$ if every solution $y$ of $(*)$ can be written as $y=c_{1}y_{1}+c_{2}y_{2}+\dots+c_{n}y_{n}$

Theorem: If $y$ is a solution of $(*)$ and $\{ y_{1},y_{2},\dots,y_{n} \}$ is a fundamental set.

taking the derivative we get the expression 

$$
y^{(n-1)} = c_{1}y^{(n-1)}(t_{0})
+\dots+
c_{2}y^{(n-1)}_{n}=y_{0}^{(n-1)}
$$
write the process as a matrix 

$$
W(y_{1},y_{2},\dots,y_{n})= \left |
\begin{matrix}
y_{1}(t_{0}) & y_{1}(t_{1})  & \dots& y_{n}(t_{0}) \\
y_{1}'(t_{0}) & y_{1}'(t_{1})  & \dots& y_{n}'(t_{0}) \\
\vdots & \vdots &  & \vdots \\
y_{1}^{(n-1)}(t_{0})  & y_{1}^{(n-1)}(t_{0}) & \dots & y_{n}^{(n-1)}(t_{0})
\end{matrix} \right |


$$
> this is then the Wronskian matrix for the *nth* derivative


- just like with other Wronskian derivatives if the result is non zero then we have a fundamental set of solutions. 

### ables theorem 
- Let $y_{1},y_{2}$ be two solutions of 
$y''+p(t)y'+g(t)y=0$ with $p(t)$ and $g(t)$ continuous on $(a,b)$ then 
$$
W(t)= \left |\begin{matrix}
y_{1} & y_{2} \\
y_{1}' & y_{2}'
\end{matrix} \right|
$$
$W(t)$ satisfies the equation $W'=p(t)W$ therefore when solving we get 

$$
\boxed {W(t)=W(t_{0})e^{-\int p(t) \, dt }}
$$

- This tells us that the Wronskian is either always zero or never zero on the interval (a,b)
	- this is because if $W(t_{0})$ is zero then the entire Wronskian is zero on the entire interval. 

- For the equation $W'=p(t)W$ 
	- for second order equations $p(t)$ is $y''+p(t)y'+q(t)y=g(t)$ for higher order equations $p(t)$ is simply a coefficient of the function order -1.
